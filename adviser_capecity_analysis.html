Instructions

This calculator is an optional tool, designed to be used by business owners to help determine the capacity of the advisers in their business to write initial advice and/or to service existing clients. The inputs contained in the tool are provided by way of example only. You are responsible for determining the relevant data to input into the tool. If the tool indicates that you "should" or "must" use particular figures or make particular assumptions , you should interpret that as guidance only as all decisions must be made by you.

Step 1

Determine the number of hours per year the adviser has available to do work for clients

In step 1 you will be determining the number of hours per year that an adviser can allocate to doing work for individual clients. This may be face to face work with the client or it may be work the adviser does for the client when the client is not present. It will include new SOA work time as well as time working with existing clients.

Enter the following data:

  1. Enter the number of weeks the adviser takes as annual leave. Usually this will be 4 weeks per year. However if the adviser has negotiated more than this enter the actual number of weeks the adviser will be on annual leave.
  2. Enter the number of weeks the adviser takes as personal leave/sick leave. Typically we allow 1 week but change this to suit the history in your business.
  3. Enter the number of weeks taken up with Public Holidays. Most states have around 10 public holidays per year so we usually use 2 weeks.
  4. Enter the number of weeks you wish to allocate to Professional Development. Consider the number of days per year attending PD days, conferences, studding for qualifications etc.
  5. If you have any other type of leave that the adviser may take over the next 12 months make allowances for that in the "Other" cell. For example this may be maternity or paternity leave. In column H you will now have calculated the number of weeks the adviser will work over the next 12 months.
  6. Enter the percentage of the adviser's time you believe they can realistically allocate to working on individual client's issues. Advisers can NOT spend 100% of their time doing client work. Their time is also spread across prospecting, attending staff meetings, meetings with referral partners, travel time and other admin work eg (emails/preparing activity reports) etc. For an employee adviser who does not have to prospect for their own leads, rarely travels to appointments and is highly efficient we often find that, at best they will spend 75% of their time doing work for individual clients. For an adviser who has to do their own prospecting and/or regularly travels to appointments use 65% to 75%. For business owner use 55% to 65% as their time is also used managing the business and staff.
    7. In column N you will now have calculated the number of hours the adviser can reasonably allocate to doing work for individual clients over the next 12 months. The next task is to calculate how much of that time will be spent writing initial advice and how much servicing existing clients.
Step 2

Determine how many hours per year the adviser will use meeting their initial advice target

In step 2 you will be determining the number of hours per year that an adviser requires to meet their initial advice targets. Note: This could be initial advice written on new clients as well as initial advice written on existing clients.

Enter the following data:

  1. Enter the different type of clients the adviser will see for the year.
  2. Enter the expected number of sales the adviser is expected to make for the full year ofr each type of initial advice
  3. Enter the typical income the adviser will earn from each Initial Advice sale. Include all revenue: fees, insurance comms, investment comms, brokerage, mortgage revenue etc. per case.In column F you will have calculated the revenue the adviser is expected to earn over the 12 month period from new sales.
  4. Enter the typical number of hours the adviser will spend writing each piece of initial advice. Some cases will take longer than others, enter your best estimate of the average time per case.In column H you will have calculated the number of hours the adviser will spend over the next 12 months to achieve their $ sales target.
  5. Enter the adviser's conversion rate. This is the conversion rate from 1st appointment to sale.In column J you will have calculated the number of prospects the adviser needs to have initial meetings with in order to meet their sales targets given not all initial meetings will lead to a sale.Column K calculates the number of initial appointments that do NOT result in sales. We need to allow for the time spent in these meetings as part of our adviser capacity calculations.
  6. Enter the number of hours that an adviser typically spends with prospects that do not proceed.
  7. In column M you will have calculated the number of total hours spent by the adviser on all the prospects that do not proceed.
    8. Column N calculates the total hours spent writing initial advice. It includes the hours spent with prospects that did not proceed as well as the hours spent with clients who did proceed. (Note: the time spent prospecting was allowed for in the "Utilisation Percentage" in the first table. Therefore we do not need to make allowance for it in this table)
Step 3

Determine the number of clients the adviser can service with the remaining available hours.

In step 3 you will determine the number of hours the adviser will need to provide the ongoing service to the existing clients they are servicing on behalf of the business.

Enter the following data:

  1. Type in the names of your practice's client segments.
  2. Enter the number of Annual Planning Meetings per client per year that are provided to clients in each segment. If clients typically only come in every second year show this by entering 0.5 meetings per year. If the clients only attend ever 3rd year enter 0.33 etc. Note: Not all clients turn up for all their scheduled Annual Planning Meetings so factor this in when entering data in this section.In column F you will have calculated the number of Planning Meetings the adviser will conduct in the next 12 months.
  3. Enter the time it takes to conduct each Annual Planning Meeting. Include preparation time, meeting time and follow up work for the annual planning meeting and if the adviser usually travels to the meetings include travel time as well.In column H you will have calculated the number of hours the adviser requires to conduct their Annual Planning Meetings with clients over the next 12 months.As well as conducting Annual Planning Meetings the adviser will provide other services to clients such as responding to emails/phone calls, any outbound calls, other services etc. We now need to allow for this additional service work.
  4. Estimate the number of hours the adviser will spend providing these additional services throughout the year. Don't think of the worst case nor of the best case. Base estimates on a typical client in each client segment.
    5. In column K you will have calculated the number of hours the adviser requires to provide the additional services to clients in each segment. conduct their Annual Planning Meetings with clients over the next 12 months.
